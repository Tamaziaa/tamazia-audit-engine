# Tamazia Architecture Research Digest: How the World's Best Build Compliance Scanners, Law-Applicability Engines, Entity Resolution, and Findings UX

This digest synthesizes how commercial compliance scanners, regtech law-modelling standards, entity-resolution/KYB products, and audit/pentest report tooling actually work, and distills concrete architecture lessons for Tamazia (URL in → legal name, jurisdictions, sector, applicable laws, and live on-page breaches with quoted proof). Sources are inline.

---

## A) How commercial compliance/consent/accessibility scanners work

**Cookiebot (Usercentrics).** The scanner crawls the site and inventories every cookie-setting mechanism — HTTP cookies, scripts, iframes, embedded video, images, pixels, and more exotic vectors like ultrasound beacons. Crucially, its classification is not a guess: each cookie-setting resource (e.g. a script) is registered and given a unique identifier, and the engine identifies/verifies it later using "consent checksums, files, paths and keyword matchings." Detected cookies are bucketed into four regulator-aligned categories — Necessary, Preferences, Statistics, Marketing — with anything unrecognized held in an explicit **Unclassified** state until reviewed rather than force-assigned. The scan output then compiles into a configuration file consumed by the consent banner, and the legal rule ("only strictly-necessary cookies before consent under GDPR") is applied to that classification. Two lessons already visible: (1) fingerprint the *resource that causes the breach*, don't just observe the symptom; (2) have a first-class "unclassified/needs-review" state instead of forcing false certainty. ([Cookiebot scan report](https://support.cookiebot.com/hc/en-us/articles/5007079527580-Understanding-the-scan-report), [what it finds](https://support.cookiebot.com/hc/en-us/articles/360004155074-What-kinds-of-cookies-and-tracking-technologies-does-Cookiebot-find), [unclassified state](https://support.cookiebot.com/hc/en-us/articles/360004177554-Check-your-unclassified-cookies-in-the-Cookiebot-Admin))

**OneTrust.** OneTrust's differentiator is a **reference knowledge base**: it scans and then auto-categorizes trackers by mapping them to *Cookiepedia*, a database of 11M+ pre-categorized technologies. Its scanner can simulate real user journeys, scan behind logins, and trigger hidden pages — i.e. it doesn't just fetch one HTML page, it *drives* the site. Its auto-blocking works by determining which script drops which cookie, then rewriting script tags to `type="text/plain"` with a category class until consent flips them to `text/javascript`. The lessons: a **curated reference database of known third parties** (trackers, ad networks, processors) is a moat and a false-positive killer; and detecting breaches on a modern SPA requires **rendering + interaction**, not a static GET. ([OneTrust auto-blocking](https://www.onetrust.com/blog/onetrust-preferencechoice-cookie-auto-blocking-technology/), [Cookie Consent product](https://www.onetrust.com/products/cookie-consent/), [KB article](https://my.onetrust.com/s/article/UUID-cdb2fcad-1640-fdbd-e432-73c5536f0bbd?language=en_US))

**axe-core (Deque) — the gold standard for false-positive discipline.** axe-core runs against a live DOM (browser/headless Chromium/Node) and returns a structured list where every violation is tied to a specific WCAG success criterion and a specific DOM node. Its architecture is instructive: each rule is a function returning **pass / fail / needs-review**, and every rule carries **tags** (WCAG version, level A/AA/AAA, or `best-practice`, plus the SC number). Its false-positive philosophy is the key export for Tamazia: **"zero false positives (bugs notwithstanding)"** — anything it cannot assert with 100% certainty is returned as `incomplete` and routed to manual review, and any false positive is treated as a *bug to be fixed*. It only checks rendered content, deliberately declining to guess on things it can't determine, which is exactly why it catches ~57% of WCAG issues automatically but never over-claims. This maps directly onto Tamazia's "live breaches with quoted proof" — a finding you can't tie to a rendered element with a quote should degrade to "needs review," not "violation." ([axe-core GitHub](https://github.com/dequelabs/axe-core), [Deque axe-core](https://www.deque.com/axe/axe-core/), [API docs](https://github.com/dequelabs/axe-core/blob/develop/doc/API.md), [ACT implementation](https://www.w3.org/WAI/standards-guidelines/act/implementations/axe-core/))

**Silktide / Monsido (now Acquia Optimize).** These are broad *web-governance* platforms — one engine, many lenses: accessibility (200+ checks vs WCAG), content QA (broken links, spelling), SEO, privacy/GDPR + cookie notices, performance, and brand/regulatory content compliance. Silktide compares page elements against recognized standards (WCAG etc.) and emits a report of specific problems + actionable recommendations. The architecture lesson: a **single crawl feeding multiple rule-lenses** is how you cover many obligation types cheaply — crawl once, evaluate many rule packs. Monsido's fate (acquired Jan 2024, brand retired Oct 2024) is also a market signal: single-purpose governance scanners consolidate; a multi-jurisdiction *legal* framing is a differentiator. ([Silktide tools](https://silktide.com/tools/), [free accessibility scan](https://silktide.com/free-accessibility-scan/), [what happened to Monsido](https://silktide.com/blog/what-happened-to-monsido/))

**How they phrase findings legally safely.** Audit practice separates **findings** (nonconformities, tiered Critical/Major/Minor) from **observations** (no clear nonconformity, improvement suggested, no formal corrective action required). Compliance scanners adopt tiered severity (Critical/High/Medium/Low) keyed to *data sensitivity, content visibility, and regulatory penalty exposure*, and pair the product with a "this is not legal advice" disclaimer that sets reader expectations and reduces the risk of an implied professional-advice relationship. For Tamazia this means: an on-page detection is a **factual observation with quoted evidence** ("no consent banner was served before `analytics.js` set `_ga`"), the mapped statute is **context**, and the risk rating is an **indicator** — never an adjudicated legal conclusion. ([audit findings categories](https://simplerqms.com/audit-findings/), [findings vs observations](https://www.airsafety.aero/regulatory-oversight/findings-and-observations), [not-legal-advice disclaimer](https://legalclarity.org/this-is-not-legal-advice-disclaimer-sample/))

## B) Law-applicability modelling (statute → machine rules)

**OSCAL (NIST).** The Open Security Controls Assessment Language is NIST's machine-readable (XML/JSON/YAML) representation of controls, baselines, and assessment results. The critical framing from the ecosystem: **OSCAL is an *enabler*, not an *enforcement engine*** — it expresses *what must be true* in interoperable form but doesn't itself check anything. ([OSCAL/NIST](https://pages.nist.gov/OSCAL/), [CSRC project](https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/open-security-controls-assessment-language))

**OPA / Rego.** Open Policy Agent with the Rego language is the de-facto "policy-as-code" enforcement layer; public Rego libraries already encode CIS, NIST, ISO 27001, SOC 2, PCI-DSS, DORA, NIS2, NERC-CIP. The important architectural insight is the **two-layer split**: OSCAL (or any obligation model) sits at the *specification* layer; OPA/Rego sits at the *evaluation* layer — and IBM's **Compliance-to-Policy (C2P)** bridge mechanically transforms OSCAL artifacts into native enforcement formats (Rego, Kyverno, AWS Config). For Tamazia: model the *law/obligation* in a declarative catalogue (the "what"), and compile/route each obligation to a *deterministic check* (the "how"). Don't conflate them. ([Rego policy libraries](https://ynotbhatc.github.io/rego_policy_libraries/), [Wiz compliance-as-code](https://www.wiz.io/academy/compliance/compliance-as-code), [C2P/OSCAL-COMPASS](https://csrc.nist.gov/csrc/media/Projects/open-security-controls-assessment-language/images-media/7.17.2024_oscal-compass-End-to-End.pdf))

**Akoma Ntoso + LegalRuleML.** Akoma Ntoso is the OASIS XML standard for the *structure* of legal documents (acts, sections, judgments) — it gives every provision a stable, addressable identity so you can cite "Act X, §Y" machine-readably. **LegalRuleML** is the complementary standard for the *normative rules/logic* extracted from those documents (obligations, permissions, prohibitions, defeasibility, jurisdiction/temporal validity). Together they separate **the text of the law** (Akoma Ntoso) from **the rule you derived from it** (LegalRuleML). This is the exact backbone Tamazia's catalogue needs: a citation layer ("act/section" for every finding, which the pen-test literature demands) plus a rule layer that carries applicability conditions. ([Akoma Ntoso (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akoma_Ntoso), [LegalRuleML paper](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/225256462_LegalRuleML_XML-Based_Rules_and_Norms), [Legis standards overview](https://www.xcential.com/legis-standards))

**Applicability / regulatory mapping in production regtech.** Vendors like Ascent and Regology build a **Regulatory Map**: product offerings × jurisdictions × regulators × regulatory content, feeding an **Obligations Inventory/Register** decomposed to the *line level* of a regulation. RegTech platforms automate capture → classification → standardization → mapping across jurisdictions. This is precisely Tamazia's "catalogue tagged by jurisdiction × sector × activity." The mature pattern is an **applicability matrix**: each obligation row carries predicate tags (jurisdiction, sector/sub-sector, activity: B2B/B2C, e-commerce, cookies, ads, data processing), and applicability is a *set-membership query* over the resolved facts about the target company, not a hand-wave. ([Ascent reg-mapping](https://www.ascentregtech.com/blog/reg-mapping-is-key-to-compliance-are-you-doing-it-effectively/), [what is regulatory mapping](https://fintech.global/2022/12/01/what-is-regulatory-mapping-and-why-is-it-important/), [Regology](https://www.regology.com/llms-txt))

## C) Entity resolution done right (URL → legal entity + jurisdictions with high confidence)

**Ground-truth registries.** The world's best KYB resolves a website to a legal entity by triangulating **authoritative registers**, never a single source:
- **GLEIF / LEI** — the global identifier for legal entities, and uniquely valuable because LEI data answers "who owns whom" with verified parent/subsidiary relationships. GLEIF+OpenCorporates now publish an open **ID-to-LEI relationship file**; >50% of the global LEI population is linked to OpenCorporates. ([GLEIF↔OpenCorporates mapping](https://www.gleif.org/en/lei-data/lei-mapping/download-oc-to-lei-relationship-files), [partnership](https://www.gleif.org/en/newsroom/press-releases/gleif-and-opencorporates-expand-partnership-to-increase-legal-entity-transparency-in-global-markets))
- **Companies House / OpenCorporates** — foundational registry facts: status, registered address, directors, industry (SIC) codes — the basis for *jurisdiction of registration*.
- **SEC EDGAR** — the **CIK** (10-digit Central Index Key, never recycled) plus current/former names and tickers for US public filers, and filings that state principal places of business. ([EDGAR APIs](https://www.sec.gov/search-filings/edgar-application-programming-interfaces), [accessing EDGAR data](https://www.sec.gov/search-filings/edgar-search-assistance/accessing-edgar-data))
- **OpenSanctions** — screening/adverse layer built on the same GLEIF/registry graph. ([GLEIF on OpenSanctions](https://www.opensanctions.org/datasets/gleif/))

The deterministic KYB verdict pattern (approve / review / EDD / reject, with risk scoring) shows the shape Tamazia wants for its *confidence* requirement: resolution isn't a boolean, it's a graded verdict backed by corroborating identifiers. ([GLEIF LEI/KYB lookup](https://apify.com/ryanclinton/gleif-lei-lookup))

**On-page signals.** schema.org `Organization` markup gives you the entity's *self-declaration*: `legalName` (official registered name, distinct from the trading `name`) and — most importantly for resolution — `sameAs`, an array of URLs to Wikidata, LinkedIn, Crunchbase, etc. `sameAs` is the single most direct lever for **entity linking**: it connects the on-site declaration to the off-site entity graph. Combine on-page signals (footer "© X Ltd", registered number, VAT ID, privacy policy controller name, physical addresses) with registry lookups to reach high confidence. ([schema.org Organization](https://schema.org/Organization), [Google Organization markup](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/structured-data/organization), [sameAs entity linking](https://www.fokal.com/ai-seo/organization-schema-markup/))

**The resolution architecture lesson:** a website is not an entity — it's a claim. Resolve it by (1) extracting on-page identity claims (legalName, company number, VAT, addresses, schema.org, WHOIS/registrant, TLD), (2) matching each against registry ground truth, (3) requiring *multiple corroborating identifiers* before asserting a jurisdiction "with 100% confidence," and (4) emitting a graded confidence with the evidence chain. Registered office ≠ places of operation — you must gather *all* offices/registrations (subsidiaries via LEI parent/child graph, multiple Companies House/EDGAR filings, address blocks in ToS/privacy pages) to claim jurisdictions "of operation," not just of incorporation.

## D) How pentest/audit report tools present findings (UX that converts)

The pentest reporting literature is remarkably consistent and directly transferable. A finding is a **structured record**, every field load-bearing:
- **Title** (clear, descriptive), **severity** (Critical/High/Medium/Low/Informational) + **CVSS**, **affected asset** (URL/host/component), **description**, **proof-of-concept evidence** (request/response captures, screenshots, sanitized data sample), **step-by-step reproduction**, **business-impact statement**, and **remediation with references**. ([Wiz pentest report](https://www.wiz.io/academy/vulnerability-management/penetration-testing-report), [Astra VAPT template](https://www.getastra.com/blog/security-audit/penetration-testing-report/), [DeepStrike structure](https://deepstrike.io/blog/penetration-testing-report))
- The line that matters most for Tamazia: **"a finding with no reproduction steps cannot be trusted or fixed with confidence… this is the line between a real penetration test and a speculative scanner alert."** Tamazia's "quoted proof" requirement *is* the reproduction step. Every breach must ship with the exact quote/element/network event that proves it, or it isn't a finding.
- **Severity ≠ raw CVSS.** Best practice explicitly says severity must reflect *real-world exploitability and business impact* — is the asset internet-facing, does it touch sensitive data — and pairs CVSS with **EPSS** (probability of exploitation). Tamazia's analogue: severity = penalty exposure × data sensitivity × how public/certain the breach is, not just "cookies bad." ([EJN reading a pentest report](https://ejnlabs.com/how-to-read-a-pen-test-report/), [Viking Cloud](https://www.vikingcloud.com/blog/penetration-testing-report))
- **Two-audience layout:** a jargon-free executive summary (overall risk, # of criticals, business impact, priorities) over technically detailed findings, and **every finding gets at least one specific fix with an identified owner.** This is the report structure that "converts" — execs act on the summary, engineers act on the reproduction + fix.

## E) "Deterministic rules + LLM assist" architectures (public engineering evidence)

The consensus pattern across academic and vendor sources: **deterministic core, LLM at the edges.**
- Early RegTech was deterministic/rule-based (formal logic, XBRL); LLMs added regulation-parsing and workflow automation but bring *transparency, interpretability, auditability* problems and struggle with rapid regulatory change. So neither alone wins. ([Compliance-to-Code paper, arXiv 2505.19804](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.19804))
- **The production pattern**: "wrap ML/LLM outputs with a *versioned rule API* whose logic is authored as a **decision table** — the model is the probabilistic expert; the table encodes policy, compliance constraints, and human-escalation triggers." Deterministic checks belong *upstream* in rule logic; LLMs provide explainability and augmentation, not the verdict. ([Rulebricks deterministic guardrails](https://rulebricks.com/blog/deterministic-guardrails-for-llms-building-safe-auditable-ai-systems), [MightyBot deterministic AI](https://mightybot.ai/blog/what-is-deterministic-ai/))
- **CommBank's ReAct policy-engine** post shows the concrete shape: an LLM agent retains agency, but a deterministic rule-based policy engine gates its actions — "not a replacement for LLM guardrails but bringing determinism to codifiable policies." ([CommBank Technology](https://medium.com/commbank-technology/enforcing-compliance-while-retaining-agency-a-rule-based-policy-engine-approach-for-react-agents-a9a8a1b4a88c), [Neota rule-based vs generative](https://neota.com/rule-based-vs-generative-ai-legal-teams/))

For Tamazia: **the breach detection and law-applicability decisions must be deterministic and reproducible; the LLM is confined to fuzzy extraction (reading a privacy policy for the controller name, classifying sector from prose, drafting the human-readable explanation) — always with its output fed back through a deterministic validator.**

## Synthesized architecture lessons for Tamazia (16)

1. **Separate the four stages into independent, individually-auditable engines: Resolve → Classify → Apply → Detect.** Entity resolution, sector classification, law applicability, and on-page breach detection each have different truth sources and failure modes. Never let a weak sector guess silently taint a confident breach finding.

2. **Model laws in two layers, à la Akoma Ntoso + LegalRuleML / OSCAL + Rego.** A *citation layer* (act, section, URL to statute — every finding must cite one, per pentest norms) and a *rule layer* (machine-evaluable predicate with applicability tags and a penalty). Keep the "what the law says" separate from "the check that detects the breach." ([LegalRuleML](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/225256462_LegalRuleML_XML-Based_Rules_and_Norms), [C2P bridge](https://csrc.nist.gov/csrc/media/Projects/open-security-controls-assessment-language/images-media/7.17.2024_oscal-compass-End-to-End.pdf))

3. **Applicability is a set-membership query over a jurisdiction × sector × activity matrix**, exactly like Ascent/Regology obligation registers. Each catalogue entry carries predicate tags; a law applies iff the resolved company facts satisfy them. This makes applicability explainable ("this applies because: EU + e-commerce + B2C"). ([Ascent](https://www.ascentregtech.com/blog/reg-mapping-is-key-to-compliance-are-you-doing-it-effectively/))

4. **Resolve entities by corroboration, not by any single source.** Triangulate on-page claims (schema.org `legalName`/`sameAs`, footer, VAT/company number, WHOIS) against GLEIF/LEI + Companies House/OpenCorporates + SEC EDGAR. Require ≥2 corroborating identifiers before asserting a jurisdiction "with 100% confidence." ([GLEIF mapping](https://www.gleif.org/en/lei-data/lei-mapping/download-oc-to-lei-relationship-files), [schema.org sameAs](https://www.fokal.com/ai-seo/organization-schema-markup/))

5. **Distinguish jurisdiction of *incorporation* from jurisdictions of *operation*.** Walk the LEI parent/child graph and collect every registered office, subsidiary, and address block in ToS/privacy/contact pages — operations create obligations that a single registration does not reveal.

6. **Emit a graded resolution verdict (like KYB's approve/review/EDD/reject), not a boolean.** Attach the evidence chain to every jurisdiction/legal-name claim so the confidence is inspectable and defensible. ([KYB deterministic verdict](https://apify.com/ryanclinton/gleif-lei-lookup))

7. **Build a curated reference database of third parties** (trackers, ad/marketing pixels, analytics, CMPs, processors) — OneTrust's Cookiepedia (11M+ entries) is the moat and the primary false-positive killer. Fingerprint the *resource* (script URL/path/hash/keyword), not just the symptom cookie. ([OneTrust/Cookiepedia](https://www.onetrust.com/blog/onetrust-preferencechoice-cookie-auto-blocking-technology/), [Cookiebot resource fingerprinting](https://support.cookiebot.com/hc/en-us/articles/5007079527580-Understanding-the-scan-report))

8. **Detect on a rendered, interacted DOM — not a static GET.** OneTrust simulates user journeys, scans behind logins, triggers hidden pages; axe-core and Silktide evaluate the live DOM. Cookie/consent/ads breaches only appear after scripts run and network calls fire, so drive a headless browser and capture the network waterfall. ([OneTrust scanning](https://www.onetrust.com/products/cookie-consent/), [axe-core DOM](https://github.com/dequelabs/axe-core))

9. **Adopt axe-core's false-positive doctrine as a product principle: three states — violation / needs-review / pass — and treat every false positive as a bug.** Anything you cannot tie to rendered evidence with certainty degrades to "needs review," never "breach." This is existential for a tool whose whole claim is "live breaches with quoted proof." ([axe-core zero-FP](https://www.deque.com/axe/axe-core/))

10. **Every finding is a structured record with a mandatory evidence quote and reproduction path**, mirroring pentest reports: title, severity, act/section citation, affected URL/element, **the quoted proof**, reproduction steps, penalty, remediation. "No reproduction = not a finding." ([Astra template](https://www.getastra.com/blog/security-audit/penetration-testing-report/), [DeepStrike](https://deepstrike.io/blog/penetration-testing-report))

11. **Severity = penalty exposure × data sensitivity × detection certainty × public visibility — not a flat CVSS-style number.** Borrow CVSS+EPSS thinking: rank by real-world impact and likelihood of enforcement, so a missing cookie banner on a high-traffic EU B2C checkout outranks a cosmetic policy gap. ([EJN pentest severity](https://ejnlabs.com/how-to-read-a-pen-test-report/))

12. **Two-audience report UX: a jargon-free executive summary over engineer-actionable findings, each with a specific fix and owner.** This is the documented "report that converts." For Tamazia, the exec view = risk score + count of criticals + top penalty exposure; the detail view = quote + statute + fix. ([Wiz report structure](https://www.wiz.io/academy/vulnerability-management/penetration-testing-report))

13. **Deterministic detection core; LLM strictly at the fuzzy edges, always validated.** Breach logic and applicability must be reproducible rule evaluations (decision tables / Rego-style), version-pinned. Confine the LLM to extraction (controller name from a privacy policy, sector from prose) and natural-language explanation, then re-validate its output deterministically. ([Rulebricks](https://rulebricks.com/blog/deterministic-guardrails-for-llms-building-safe-auditable-ai-systems), [CommBank](https://medium.com/commbank-technology/enforcing-compliance-while-retaining-agency-a-rule-based-policy-engine-approach-for-react-agents-a9a8a1b4a88c))

14. **Version everything: the law catalogue, the rule logic, the reference DB, and each scan.** Regtech's core pain is regulatory change; audits require you to reproduce *what the rule was on the scan date*. Store law versions with effective/temporal validity (LegalRuleML supports this) so a finding is reproducible months later. ([RegTech change management](https://www.ascentregtech.com/our-difference/change-management/))

15. **Legally-safe language by construction: findings are factual observations + risk indicators, never legal adjudications.** Use audit-grade phrasing (observation vs finding), attach a persistent "not legal advice / informational, verify with counsel" disclaimer, and phrase output as "detected X; this may implicate Act §Y (penalty Z)" rather than "you are in breach." This is both legally protective and more accurate given detection uncertainty. ([findings vs observations](https://www.airsafety.aero/regulatory-oversight/findings-and-observations), [not-legal-advice disclaimer](https://legalclarity.org/this-is-not-legal-advice-disclaimer-sample/))

16. **One crawl, many rule-lenses (Silktide/Monsido model).** Crawl and render the site once, then run independent rule packs (cookies/consent, e-commerce disclosures, ads/marketing, accessibility, privacy-policy presence) tagged to the applicable-law set. This keeps cost linear as you add sectors/jurisdictions and lets you ship sector packs incrementally. Also note the market signal: standalone governance scanners consolidated (Monsido → Acquia); Tamazia's multi-jurisdiction *legal-applicability* framing is the defensible differentiator over yet-another accessibility/cookie checker. ([Silktide tools](https://silktide.com/tools/), [Monsido consolidation](https://silktide.com/blog/what-happened-to-monsido/))
