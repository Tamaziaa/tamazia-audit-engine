## PART A — LLM ACCURACY ARCHITECTURE FOR A LEGAL-CLAIMS PRODUCT

### The threat model you are designing against (why "unlikely" is not good enough)

The empirical baseline for legal AI is worse than intuition suggests. Stanford RegLab/HAI's preregistered study of *commercial, RAG-backed* legal tools found Lexis+ AI produced incorrect information **>17%** of the time and Westlaw AI-Assisted Research **>34%** of the time, versus GPT-4 at ~43% — i.e., retrieval helps but does not solve it, and the headline finding was "hallucinate in 1 out of 6 (or more) queries" ([Stanford HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu/news/ai-trial-legal-models-hallucinate-1-out-6-or-more-benchmarking-queries)). A separate citation-grounding study measured **13–21%** hallucinated legal *citations* across five systems ([arXiv Citation Grounding](https://arxiv.org/html/2606.00898)). The design implication: a single well-prompted strong model is a ~1-in-6 liability generator. You must architect so that a false legal claim is *structurally blocked*, not merely improbable.

The core mental shift: **the LLM is a retrieval-and-selection engine, not an author of facts.** Every factual assertion in output must trace to a verbatim span in a retrieved source, and anything that cannot be traced must be withheld.

### DO / DON'T TABLE

| # | DO | DON'T | Source |
|---|----|----|--------|
| 1 | Force *grounded generation*: constrain every factual claim to embed verbatim statutory/case phrases as evidence markers; prohibit citing anything outside the retrieved set. | Let the model "recall" a statute or holding from parametric memory — that is exactly where the 13–34% error lives. | [Citation Grounding, arXiv 2606.00898](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.00898); [Stanford HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu/news/ai-trial-legal-models-hallucinate-1-out-6-or-more-benchmarking-queries) |
| 2 | Treat the task as **extraction/selection**, not generation: the LLM may only SELECT from retrieved candidate spans, and output IDs/offsets, never free-authored facts. Confidence-guided copy-based decoding (CoCoLex) literally copies from source under confidence control. | Ask the model to "write the answer" and then check it afterward — post-hoc checking has recall gaps; prevention beats detection. | [CoCoLex, arXiv 2508.05534](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2508.05534) |
| 3 | Verify each output sentence by **NLI entailment** against its cited premise (DeBERTa-v3-large/MNLI-class model, or a purpose-built verifier like Luna); keep only claims labeled *entailment*. | Trust fluency as a proxy for faithfulness; a well-formed sentence that is *neutral* w.r.t. the source is a hallucination. | [Nature semantic entropy](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07421-0); [Luna, arXiv 2406.00975](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2406.00975); [Google, Sources of LLM Hallucination in NLI](https://research.google/pubs/sources-of-llm-hallucination-in-natural-language-inference/) |
| 4 | **Abstain by default.** Adopt selective prediction: answer only the subset the system is confident on; reward "I don't know"; return "insufficient authority" rather than a guess. | Force an answer on every query (standard eval regime), which "causes confident mistakes and hallucinations." | [SelectLLM/OpenReview](https://openreview.net/forum?id=JJPAy8mvrQ); [I-CALM, arXiv 2604.03904](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.03904v1) |
| 5 | **String-match every quote back to the corpus.** After generation, take each quoted span and require an exact (normalized) substring hit in the cited document; drop non-matches. | Assume the model quoted accurately — models paraphrase inside quotation marks and fabricate pincites. | [Stanford HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu/news/ai-trial-legal-models-hallucinate-1-out-6-or-more-benchmarking-queries) (citation/quote fabrication); [Citation Grounding](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.00898) |
| 6 | **Enforce structured outputs with constrained decoding** (OpenAI Strict mode / grammar-masked tokens), so every claim ships with mandatory fields: `source_id`, `char_span`, `verbatim_quote`, `entailment_label`. Missing field ⇒ cannot emit. | Rely on prompt-only "please return JSON." OpenAI reports ~100% schema-following with constrained decoding vs <40% with prompting. | [OpenAI Structured Outputs guide](https://logic.inc/resources/structured-outputs-guide); [JSONSchemaBench, arXiv 2501.10868](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2501.10868) |
| 7 | Use **schema to enforce shape, then a separate semantic gate** (NLI + quote match). Recognize the boundary: structured outputs guarantee *shape*, not *truth*. | Conflate "valid JSON" with "true claim." Schema conformance says nothing about semantic correctness. | [Aidan Cooper, Constrained Decoding](https://www.aidancooper.co.uk/constrained-decoding/); [Logic.inc](https://logic.inc/resources/structured-outputs-guide) |
| 8 | For the *judgment* layer, use a **diverse jury / ensemble with a rejection (minority-veto) rule** — weak judges have high true-positive but very low true-negative rates (<25%), so requiring unanimity-to-accept and any-veto-to-reject raises precision. | Use one LLM judge, or average many near-identical models. Single-judge scores are "insufficiently stable"; a strong model diluted by weak clones underperforms the strong model alone. | [orq.ai LLM juries](https://orq.ai/blog/llm-juries-in-practice); [Weak LLMs Judging Strong LLMs](https://www.emergentmind.com/topics/weak-llms-judging-strong-llms); [Ensemble survey, arXiv 2503.13505](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.13505) |
| 9 | Mitigate **LLM-as-judge bias** explicitly: position-swap, hide length/formatting cues, chain-of-thought rationale before verdict, calibrate scoring. | Let the judge reward "longer, authoritative-looking, well-formatted" answers — a bias consistent across *all* judges. | [Judging the Judges, arXiv 2604.23178](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.23178); [CalibraEval, arXiv 2410.15393](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2410.15393) |
| 10 | Build a **versioned golden set** from real production failures (≥60% production-sampled, 50–100 cases, 40–60% core + 15–25% edge), and gate every prompt/model/retriever change on it in CI. | Ship synthetic-only golden sets (judge calibrates to synthesis style, not user behavior) or change prompts without a regression gate. | [Golden set design guide](https://futureagi.com/blog/llm-eval-golden-set-design-2026/); [Arize golden dataset](https://arize.com/resource/golden-dataset/); [Statsig](https://www.statsig.com/perspectives/golden-datasets-evaluation-standards) |
| 11 | Treat **temperature-0 ≠ deterministic.** If you need reproducibility for audit/legal defensibility, use batch-invariant kernels and pin the full stack; log inputs+outputs+model build. | Assume `temperature=0` gives you bit-identical, defensible reproduction. Nondeterminism comes from batch-size-dependent reduction kernels, not just sampling. | [Thinking Machines, Defeating Nondeterminism](https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/defeating-nondeterminism-in-llm-inference/); [arXiv 2506.09501](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.09501) |
| 12 | Prefer **hybrid retrieval + contextual compression** so only relevant, high-quality spans reach the model; grounding score improves and citation density drops. | Dump large context and hope; poor retrieval is upstream of most "grounded" hallucinations. | [RAG hallucination review, MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/13/5/856); [Citation Grounding](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.00898) |

### THE 5 PATTERNS THAT MAKE A FALSE LEGAL CLAIM *STRUCTURALLY IMPOSSIBLE* (not merely unlikely)

The distinction: an *unlikely* false claim is one a model was discouraged from making; a *structurally impossible* one is a claim the system **physically cannot emit** because a hard gate rejects it. Each pattern below is a gate that fails *closed*.

**Pattern 1 — Closed-book prohibition / retrieval-gated emission.** The generation path has no access to parametric "facts" as output. The model receives only retrieved candidate spans and may emit a claim *only if* it references a `source_id` present in that retrieval set. Enforced by constrained decoding: the grammar only permits `source_id` tokens drawn from the injected candidate list, so a citation to a non-retrieved authority is *unrepresentable*. This is the mechanism behind citation-grounded generation that "prohibits citing provisions outside retrieved results" ([Citation Grounding, arXiv 2606.00898](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.00898); [OpenAI Structured Outputs / CFG masking](https://logic.inc/resources/structured-outputs-guide)).

**Pattern 2 — Verbatim quote re-verification (exact string match back to corpus).** Every quoted span must survive a normalized exact-substring check against the cited document *before* the claim is allowed into output. A fabricated or paraphrased "quote" produces a match miss and the claim is dropped. This converts the most common legal failure (fake quotes/pincites, per Stanford) from a probabilistic risk into a deterministic reject ([Stanford HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu/news/ai-trial-legal-models-hallucinate-1-out-6-or-more-benchmarking-queries)).

**Pattern 3 — NLI entailment gate per claim.** Each atomic claim is the *hypothesis*; its cited span is the *premise*; a fine-tuned entailment model (DeBERTa-MNLI class, or Luna-style eval model) must return `entailment` — `neutral` and `contradiction` are both rejected. Because the gate scores every sentence independently against its own source, an unsupported inference cannot pass even if the surrounding text is fluent ([Nature 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07421-0); [Luna](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2406.00975)). Caveat to design around: NLI models themselves hallucinate via memorization/lexical-overlap bias, so use a verifier NLI model *not* trained on your corpus and audit its calibration ([Google Research](https://research.google/pubs/sources-of-llm-hallucination-in-natural-language-inference/)).

**Pattern 4 — Abstain-by-default with a confidence floor (selective prediction).** The default output is "insufficient authority / escalate to counsel." A claim is released only if it clears (a) a retrieval-support threshold, (b) the NLI gate, and (c) a calibrated confidence floor. Coverage is deliberately traded for risk: the system answers the subset it is likely correct on and abstains elsewhere. Note the research nuance — *calibration alone is neither sufficient nor necessary* for good selective prediction, so combine confidence scoring + uncertainty estimation + explicit abstention rewards rather than relying on a single confidence number ([SelectLLM/OpenReview](https://openreview.net/forum?id=JJPAy8mvrQ); [I-CALM](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.03904v1); [Uncertainty-Aware Abstention w/ provable guarantees, arXiv 2607.04430](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.04430)).

**Pattern 5 — Independent adjudication jury with veto-to-reject.** A separate panel of *diverse* judge models (not clones of the generator) verifies the final claim set; acceptance requires consensus and *any* judge's veto rejects. This exploits the measured asymmetry that weak judges have high true-positive but very low true-negative rates — so a rejection signal is far more trustworthy than an approval, making veto-to-reject the correct polarity. Pair with position-swapping and length/format-blinding to strip judge bias ([orq.ai juries](https://orq.ai/blog/llm-juries-in-practice); [emergentmind minority-veto](https://www.emergentmind.com/topics/weak-llms-judging-strong-llms); [Judging the Judges](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.23178)).

**Why these five compose into structural impossibility:** they are *independent, fail-closed AND-gates*. A false claim must simultaneously (1) reference a retrieved source, (2) match a real verbatim quote, (3) be entailed by that source, (4) clear the confidence floor, and (5) survive unanimous jury approval. The probability of a false claim passing all five is the *product* of five independent escape probabilities — and because gates 1–2 are deterministic string/set operations (not probabilistic), whole classes of error (fabricated citations, fake quotes) have escape probability *zero*, not merely small. Everything the gates reject routes to abstention, so the failure mode is "refuses to answer," never "confidently wrong." The residual risk that remains — a claim that is genuinely supported by a real, correctly-quoted, entailing source but is nonetheless the *wrong* legal answer for the question — is a *retrieval/relevance* problem, addressed by Pattern 12 (hybrid retrieval) + the golden-set regression gate, and is the correct place to concentrate human review.

## PART B — MULTI-AGENT CODING ORCHESTRATION FOR PRECISION

### What the primary sources actually say

**Anthropic's orchestrator-worker pattern.** In "Building Effective AI Agents," Anthropic deliberately distinguishes *workflows* (predefined code paths) from *agents* (models directing their own process), and warns against complexity for its own sake — start simple, add agents only when the task genuinely demands it. The orchestrator-workers workflow is defined as "a central LLM dynamically breaks down tasks, delegates them to worker LLMs, and synthesizes their results," recommended precisely for coding "where you can't predict the subtasks needed" ([Anthropic, Building Effective Agents](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents); [cookbook orchestrator_workers](https://github.com/anthropics/anthropic-cookbook/blob/main/patterns/agents/orchestrator_workers.ipynb)). This is the shape the user's "Rob master agent + specialists" wants.

**Anthropic's multi-agent research system** gives the hard numbers and the caveats: lead agent (Opus) + parallel subagents (Sonnet) with isolated context windows "outperformed single-agent Claude Opus 4 by **90.2%**" on their research eval; but multi-agent systems "use **15× more tokens** than typical chats," token usage "explains ~80% of performance variance," prompt engineering was "the primary lever," and *without detailed task descriptions "agents tend to duplicate work, leave gaps, or miss critical information"* ([Anthropic, How we built our multi-agent research system](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system); [Simon Willison's notes](https://simonwillison.net/2025/Jun/14/multi-agent-research-system/)). Critically, Anthropic notes multi-agent shines for *breadth-first, read-heavy, parallelizable* work — and is a *poor* fit for tasks that need tight shared state, which coding often does.

**Why swarms fail — the empirical taxonomy.** UC Berkeley's MAST (Multi-Agent System failure taxonomy, from "Why Do Multi-Agent LLM Systems Fail?", 1600+ annotated traces across 7 frameworks, inter-annotator κ=0.88) is the authoritative map: **14 failure modes in 3 buckets — specification & system design 41.8%, inter-agent misalignment 36.9%, task verification 21.3%.** Named modes include *disobey task spec (11.8%), step repetition (15.7%), unaware of termination conditions (12.4%), loss of conversation history (2.8%)* ([arXiv 2503.13657](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.13657); [MAST GitHub](https://github.com/multi-agent-systems-failure-taxonomy/MAST); [IBM×Berkeley IT-Bench+MAST](https://huggingface.co/blog/ibm-research/itbenchandmast)). The one-line takeaway from the practitioner write-ups: **~79% of failures trace to bad specifications and broken coordination**, and *"independent validation is the most underused reliability mechanism"* ([Galileo](https://galileo.ai/blog/multi-agent-llm-systems-fail); [orq.ai](https://orq.ai/blog/why-do-multi-agent-llm-systems-fail)).

**Claude Code subagents** are the concrete mechanism: each subagent has "its own distinct system prompt, a curated set of tool permissions, and an isolated context window," reports back "a short summary, then disappears." Anthropic's single most-emphasized practice: *"verification — giving Claude a way to check its own output… If you only adopt one practice, make it that one."* Roles like Product Spec / Architect / Implementer / Tester can be chained via hooks with scoped tools ([Claude Code best practices](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/best-practices); [claude.com subagents blog](https://claude.com/blog/subagents-in-claude-code); [support power-user tips](https://support.claude.com/en/articles/14554000-claude-code-power-user-tips)).

**The generator/verifier separation** is the load-bearing insight from the coding-agent literature: *"the agent that generated the change should not grade its own homework"* — a *separate* verifier checks tests, diffs, requirements, and failure modes; acceptance is conditional on passing an *explicit test suite*, with error messages fed back to create a self-correcting loop (TDAD/TAG). But verification is not a silver bullet: "The Verification Horizon" shows reward/verifier signals can be gamed, so verifiers must co-evolve with generators and combine executable tests + quality filtering + behavior monitoring ([Ralph Workflow verification patterns](https://ralphworkflow.com/blog/verification-patterns-for-ai-generated-code); [TDAD, arXiv 2603.17973](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.17973v2); [Verification Horizon, arXiv 2606.26300](https://arxiv.org/html/2606.26300); [Scaling Agentic Verifier, arXiv 2602.04254](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.04254)).

### THE 10 RULES FOR AN AGENT FLEET THAT CANNOT SHIP AN UNVERIFIED CHANGE

**Rule 1 — CI is the only arbiter of "done"; no agent self-certifies.** A change is merged only when an external, deterministic gate (test suite + typecheck + lint + the Part-A-style eval golden set) passes. Agent self-reports of success are explicitly disallowed as merge signals, because "the system reports success even though the actual task objective hasn't been met" is a top failure mode. The generator never grades its own homework — a *separate* verifier/CI does ([Ralph Workflow](https://ralphworkflow.com/blog/verification-patterns-for-ai-generated-code); [Galileo](https://galileo.ai/blog/multi-agent-llm-systems-fail); [TAG, arXiv 2607.02615](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.02615)).

**Rule 2 — Every subtask ships with a machine-checkable acceptance spec *before* work starts.** Since disobey-task-spec (11.8%) + under-specification drive ~79% of failures, "Rob" must decompose each unit into an explicit spec: inputs, expected outputs, and the exact tests/oracle that define success. Anthropic: without detailed task descriptions agents "duplicate work, leave gaps, or miss critical information" ([MAST, arXiv 2503.13657](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.13657); [Anthropic multi-agent](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system)).

**Rule 3 — One shared ground truth, one writer.** The repo + test suite + spec docs are the single source of truth; sub-agents read freely but only the orchestrator (or a serialized merge step) writes to shared state. This directly attacks inter-agent misalignment (36.9%) and "loss of conversation history"/context divergence — agents drift when the initial goal is open to interpretation or when they hold divergent copies of state ([MAST](https://github.com/multi-agent-systems-failure-taxonomy/MAST); [orq.ai](https://orq.ai/blog/why-do-multi-agent-llm-systems-fail)).

**Rule 4 — Generator/critic/adversarial-verifier separation, always three distinct roles.** Implementer writes code; an independent Critic reviews the diff against the spec; an adversarial Verifier tries to *break* it (writes failing tests, probes edge/failure modes). Verifier-generated inputs "can surpass ground-truth test cases" in discriminative power. The critic and verifier must be different context/instances from the implementer ([Scaling Agentic Verifier, arXiv 2602.04254](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.04254); [Claude Code best practices — verification](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/best-practices)).

**Rule 5 — Isolated context per specialist; hand off summaries + artifacts, never raw scratch context.** Use Claude Code subagents' isolated windows: heavy reading/exploration happens in a sub-context and returns a short, structured summary, keeping the orchestrator's context clean. This is both a cost lever (multi-agent burns 15× tokens) and a correctness lever (prevents context pollution/divergence). Handoffs must be *validated artifacts* (a diff, a passing test, a spec-conformance report), because *unverified handoffs* are a named failure class ([Claude Code subagents](https://claude.com/blog/subagents-in-claude-code); [Anthropic multi-agent](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system)).

**Rule 6 — Explicit termination conditions on every loop.** "Unaware of termination conditions" is 12.4% and "step repetition" 15.7% of failures. Each agent loop needs a hard stop: max iterations, a definition-of-done tied to the CI gate, and detection of no-progress cycles. A 24×7 tool loop *without* termination guards is a documented way to burn tokens and ship nothing ([MAST, arXiv 2503.13657](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.13657)).

**Rule 7 — Scoped, least-privilege tools per role; write/deploy tools gated behind CI.** Give each specialist only the tools its role needs (Architect: read + design; Implementer: edit + test; nobody gets merge/deploy directly). Anthropic's subagent model governs exactly through "scoped tools and permissions." The merge/deploy capability belongs to the CI gate, not to any agent ([Claude Code best practices](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/best-practices); [PubNub subagent best practices](https://www.pubnub.com/blog/best-practices-for-claude-code-sub-agents/)).

**Rule 8 — Start with the simplest architecture that works; add agents only when the task is genuinely parallel/unpredictable.** Anthropic explicitly warns against multi-agent complexity for its own sake and lists "over-engineered planning / monolithic agents / missing observability" as antipatterns. Multi-agent is worth its 15× token cost only for breadth-first, parallelizable work; tightly-coupled sequential coding steps often belong in a single context ([Anthropic, Building Effective Agents](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents); [AgentPatterns.ai](https://www.agentpatterns.ai/agent-design/anthropic-effective-agents-framework/)).

**Rule 9 — Full observability: log every trace, handoff, and gate decision.** MAST-style failure attribution is only possible if you can replay traces; "missing observability" is a named antipattern and monitoring frameworks (LumiMAS-style) exist because silent failures accumulate. Every agent action, tool call, and verifier verdict is logged with the inputs that produced it — this is also your legal/audit trail. Remember Rule 11 of Part A: pin the stack, because temp-0 is not reproducible by default ([LumiMAS, arXiv 2508.12412](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2508.12412); [Thinking Machines](https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/defeating-nondeterminism-in-llm-inference/)).

**Rule 10 — Regression-gate the fleet itself on a versioned eval set; a PR that regresses quality does not merge.** Treat prompts, agent role definitions, and tool configs as versioned code: every change triggers an eval run against a golden set built ≥60% from real production failures, and merges block on regression past threshold. Verifiers must co-evolve with generators (else reward-hacking), so the golden set grows from every escaped bug ([golden set design](https://futureagi.com/blog/llm-eval-golden-set-design-2026/); [LLM regression testing](https://futureagi.com/glossary/llm-regression-testing/); [Verification Horizon](https://arxiv.org/html/2606.26300)).

**Meta-principle tying B together:** an agent fleet that "cannot ship an unverified change" is one where *shipping* is defined as *passing an external deterministic gate the agents do not control* (Rule 1), every unit has a *pre-committed machine-checkable spec* (Rule 2), *generation and verification are separated* (Rule 4), *handoffs carry validated artifacts not raw context* (Rule 5), and *the whole system is itself regression-gated* (Rule 10). Autonomy is bounded by verification, exactly as Anthropic frames it: give the model a way to check its own output, and make that check the thing that decides.

## Cross-cutting synthesis (A ↔ B share one architecture)

Both halves reduce to the same principle: **separate the fallible generator from an independent, fail-closed verifier, and define "acceptable output" as "passed the verifier," never "the model said so."** In A the verifier is a stack of deterministic gates (retrieval-set membership, exact quote match, NLI entailment, confidence floor, jury veto) whose composition drives the escape probability of a false legal claim toward zero. In B the verifier is CI + an adversarial critic agent whose green light is the only definition of "merged." The "Rob" orchestrator's real job is not to write code or claims — it is to *decompose work into units with pre-committed acceptance tests and route every unit through verification*. The single highest-leverage investment for the user is the **golden/eval set** (shared by both A and B): it is the ground truth that turns "the agents seem to be working" into "the fleet provably has not regressed," and it is, per the sources, "the single most important asset" — more important than model choice or framework.
